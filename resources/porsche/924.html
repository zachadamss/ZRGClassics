Porsche 924 (1976-1988)

Common issues, symptoms, and solutions for the 924 platform

Timing Belt Failure

Symptoms: Engine won't start, loud slapping noise, bent valves after failure, visible belt cracking or fraying

The 924's Audi-derived 2.0L inline-4 (and later 2.5L in the 924S) uses a timing belt to drive the camshaft. This is an interference engine, meaning if the belt breaks, the pistons will contact the valves, causing severe engine damage. The original service interval was 60,000 miles, but most experts recommend 30,000-40,000 miles or every 4 years due to belt age degradation. The 924 Turbo and 924S (with the 944 engine) also require balance shaft belt replacement at the same interval.

Common Fixes

  • Replace timing belt every 30,000-40,000 miles or 4 years (whichever comes first)
  • Replace water pump at the same time (driven by timing belt)
  • Replace timing belt tensioner and idler rollers
  • On 924S/Turbo: replace balance shaft belt simultaneously
  • Inspect and replace front crankshaft and camshaft seals if leaking
  • Use OEM or high-quality belts (Gates, Continental) - avoid cheap alternatives

Sources: Rennlist, Pelican Parts

CIS Fuel Injection Problems

Symptoms: Hard starting (especially when cold or hot), rough idle, hesitation under acceleration, poor fuel economy, flooding, black smoke

The 924 uses Bosch K-Jetronic Continuous Injection System (CIS), a mechanical fuel injection system that was advanced for its time but is now 40+ years old. The system relies on precise fuel pressures, a mechanical fuel distributor, and vacuum-controlled warm-up regulator. Age causes the fuel distributor diaphragm to harden, injectors to clog or leak, control pressure regulator to fail, and fuel accumulator to lose pressure. Many mechanics are unfamiliar with CIS, making diagnosis challenging.

Common Fixes

  • Test and set system pressures (control pressure, system pressure, residual pressure)
  • Replace warm-up regulator (WUR) if control pressure is incorrect
  • Clean or replace fuel injectors (rebuild kits available)
  • Replace fuel accumulator if car is hard to hot-start
  • Check fuel pump delivery volume and pressure
  • Inspect air flow sensor plate for smooth movement and proper centering
  • Replace all rubber fuel lines (ethanol in modern fuel degrades old rubber)
  • Consider CIS specialist rebuild of fuel distributor if basic repairs don't help

Sources: 924Board, Pelican Parts

Clutch Cable and Clutch System Wear

Symptoms: Heavy clutch pedal, clutch slipping, difficulty shifting, clutch pedal sticks down, grinding when shifting

The 924 uses a cable-operated clutch system running through the central tunnel to the rear-mounted transaxle. The clutch cable stretches with age and use, and the guide tube in the tunnel can wear, causing increased friction. The throwout bearing and clutch fork pivot ball are also high-wear items. Because the transaxle is at the rear, clutch replacement is more involved than on front-engine/front-transaxle cars, requiring either engine removal or transaxle removal (transaxle removal is typically preferred).

Common Fixes

  • Adjust clutch cable to restore proper free play
  • Replace clutch cable if stretched beyond adjustment or frayed
  • Lubricate cable guide tube if pedal feel is poor
  • When replacing clutch, always replace throwout bearing, pilot bearing, and clutch fork
  • Consider upgraded clutch kits (Sachs, LuK) for spirited driving
  • Inspect flywheel for heat spots and have resurfaced if necessary

Sources: Rennlist, 924Board

Rust and Corrosion

Symptoms: Bubbling paint, visible rust holes, soft floors, leaking trunk or cabin, rusty battery box

The 924 is prone to rust in several well-known areas. The front fenders rust behind the headlights and at the lower edges. The battery box (in the front trunk) corrodes from acid exposure. Door bottoms and rocker panels trap moisture. The rear hatch area around the seal is vulnerable. Floor pans can rust from the inside due to clogged drain holes. Cars from northern climates or coastal areas are particularly affected. Rust in structural areas like the front frame rails is a serious safety concern.

Common Fixes

  • Inspect battery box regularly and neutralize acid; replace if severely corroded
  • Check and clear all drain holes (doors, rocker panels, floor plugs)
  • Replace front fenders if rust is extensive (reproduction fenders available)
  • Weld in repair panels for floor pan rust
  • Apply rust prevention coating (POR-15, Eastwood, etc.) to cleaned areas
  • Treat cavity areas with rust prevention spray (Fluid Film, Waxoyl)
  • Check frame rails carefully - significant rust here may make the car uneconomical to repair

Sources: 924Board, Rennlist

Electrical System Issues

Symptoms: Intermittent no-start, dead gauges, flickering lights, non-functional windows/mirrors, fuel pump runs constantly

The 924's electrical system, while simpler than modern cars, suffers from 40+ years of age. The main culprits are the DME/fuel pump relay (an early Porsche weak point), corroded ground connections, and degraded wiring insulation. The combination relay on the fuse box controls multiple functions and commonly fails. The instrument cluster uses printed circuit boards that can develop cracks. Window switches and mirror controls use contacts that wear out.

Common Fixes

  • Replace DME/fuel pump relay with new OEM unit (not cheap aftermarket)
  • Clean and tighten all ground connections (battery, engine, body)
  • Inspect and repair wiring under the hood (heat damage common)
  • Replace combination relay if multiple electrical functions are affected
  • Repair or replace instrument cluster circuit board for gauge issues
  • Replace ignition switch if experiencing intermittent starting issues
  • Check door wiring harness where it enters door (flexing causes breaks)

Sources: 924Board, Pelican Parts

Cooling System Failures

Symptoms: Overheating, coolant leaks, heater not working, temperature gauge fluctuation, steam from engine bay

The 924's cooling system works hard, especially on turbocharged models. The water pump is driven by the timing belt and should be replaced with every belt service. The radiator's plastic end tanks become brittle with age. Thermostat failure can cause overheating or prevent the engine from reaching operating temperature. The heater core can leak or clog. Cooling system hoses, now decades old, can collapse internally even if they look fine externally.

Common Fixes

  • Replace water pump with every timing belt service
  • Replace thermostat (use OEM temperature rating for proper operation)
  • Upgrade to aluminum or modern radiator if plastic tanks are cracking
  • Replace all coolant hoses (including heater hoses)
  • Flush cooling system and use proper G05 or equivalent coolant
  • On turbo models: inspect turbo coolant lines and oil cooler
  • Check fan clutch or electric fan operation

Sources: Rennlist, Pelican Parts

Transaxle and Differential Issues

Symptoms: Grinding when shifting, popping out of gear, whining noise from rear, difficulty engaging reverse, oil leaks at rear

The 924 uses a rear-mounted transaxle (Audi-derived in early cars, Porsche-designed in later models) that combines the transmission and differential. Common issues include worn synchros (especially 2nd gear), tired shift linkage, and differential wear. The transaxle mount, or "donut," deteriorates and affects shift quality. Oil leaks from output shaft seals are common on high-mileage cars. The 924's transaxle is generally robust but requires proper fluid changes.

Common Fixes

  • Change transaxle fluid regularly (every 30,000 miles) with correct GL-4 gear oil
  • Replace transaxle mount ("donut") if shift quality is poor
  • Rebuild or replace shift linkage bushings for precise shifting
  • For synchro wear: rebuild transaxle or find quality used replacement
  • Replace output shaft seals if leaking
  • On high-mileage cars: inspect differential ring and pinion for wear

Sources: 924Board, Rennlist

Suspension and Steering Wear

Symptoms: Clunking over bumps, vague steering, uneven tire wear, wandering at highway speed, squeaking from front end

After 35-45 years, nearly every 924 needs suspension attention. The front uses MacPherson struts with lower control arms. Common wear items include strut mounts (cause clunking and poor handling), control arm bushings, tie rod ends, and ball joints. The rear uses a semi-trailing arm design with bushings that wear. The steering rack can develop play and leak. The original Bilstein or Boge shocks are likely long past their service life.

Common Fixes

  • Replace strut mounts (bearing and rubber mount)
  • Install new shocks/struts (Bilstein, Koni, KW available)
  • Replace all control arm bushings (polyurethane available for performance)
  • Replace tie rod ends and check steering rack for play
  • Rebuild or replace steering rack if leaking or worn
  • Replace rear trailing arm bushings
  • Perform full alignment after suspension work

Sources: 924Board, Pelican Parts

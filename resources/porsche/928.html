Porsche 928 (1977-1995)

Common issues, symptoms, and solutions for the 928 platform

Timing Belt Failure

Symptoms: Engine won't start, catastrophic engine noise, bent valves, visible belt damage through inspection hole

The 928's V8 engine (M28 in early cars, later variations through the S4 and GTS) is an interference engine that uses a timing belt to drive the camshafts. Belt failure results in piston-to-valve contact and catastrophic engine damage. The 928 has a single belt that drives both camshafts via a complex path around multiple rollers. The belt service is more complex than on the 944 due to the V8 configuration. Original service interval was 60,000 miles, but 30,000-40,000 miles or 4 years is the modern recommendation due to belt age degradation.

Common Fixes

  • Replace timing belt every 30,000-40,000 miles or 4 years (whichever first)
  • Replace ALL timing belt rollers and tensioner (multiple rollers on V8)
  • Replace water pump at the same time
  • Replace front camshaft and crankshaft seals
  • Inspect and replace belt cover gaskets
  • Use only high-quality OEM or premium belts (Gates, Continental)
  • This is a complex job - budget 8-12 hours labor at a specialist shop

Sources: Rennlist, 928 Specialists

Cam Tower Gasket and Oil Leaks

Symptoms: Oil leaks at top of engine, burning oil smell, oil on exhaust manifolds, low oil level, smoke from engine bay

The 928 V8 is notorious for oil leaks, primarily from the cam tower (cam cover) gaskets. These gaskets are located at the top of each cylinder bank and seal the camshaft area. They harden with age and heat, causing oil to leak onto the exhaust manifolds, creating smoke and fire risk. The valve cover gaskets (above the cam towers) also leak. Additionally, the cylinder head gaskets, oil pan gasket, and various other seals deteriorate. A leaking 928 is the norm rather than the exception for high-mileage cars.

Common Fixes

  • Replace cam tower gaskets with updated Porsche gaskets
  • Replace valve cover gaskets at the same time
  • Inspect and replace cylinder head gaskets if leaking
  • Replace rear main seal if leaking (requires transmission removal)
  • Replace oil pan gasket if leaking
  • Replace all oil cooler lines and seals
  • Use proper torque specifications to avoid gasket damage

Sources: Rennlist, 928 International

Engine Wiring Harness Deterioration

Symptoms: Random misfires, check engine light, running issues, no-start conditions, sensors not reading correctly

The 928's engine wiring harness is routed through a hot area near the engine and uses insulation that becomes brittle and cracks with age and heat. This is especially problematic on S4 and later models with more complex engine management. Cracked insulation causes short circuits, intermittent connections, and ground faults that create a wide variety of drivability issues. Diagnosing electrical problems becomes extremely difficult when the harness is deteriorated, as multiple faults can occur.

Common Fixes

  • Inspect harness for cracked or brittle insulation
  • Rewrap harness with proper heat-resistant tape or conduit
  • Repair individual broken wires with proper soldering and heat shrink
  • Replace entire engine wiring harness (rebuilt harnesses available from specialists)
  • Add heat shielding to protect new harness from exhaust heat
  • Clean and protect all electrical connectors with dielectric grease

Sources: 928 Specialists, Rennlist

Automatic Transmission Issues

Symptoms: Harsh shifting, slipping, delayed engagement, no movement in gear, fluid leaks, overheating warning

Most 928s were sold with automatic transmissions (Mercedes 4-speed or later Aisin-Warner 4-speed). These transmissions are generally robust but require proper maintenance and develop problems with age. Common issues include worn valve body, torque converter failure, and failed seals causing leaks. The transmission operates at high temperatures and requires proper cooling. Overheating from a failed cooler or clogged lines can cause rapid transmission failure. The transmission shares fluid with the torque tube.

Common Fixes

  • Regular fluid and filter changes (every 30,000 miles)
  • Flush transmission cooler and lines
  • Replace transmission cooler if clogged or leaking
  • Rebuild or replace valve body for shift quality issues
  • Replace torque converter if shuddering or failing to lock up
  • Full transmission rebuild for severe issues
  • Use correct Mercedes or Porsche-spec ATF

Sources: Rennlist, 928 International

Cooling System Failures

Symptoms: Overheating, coolant leaks, white smoke from tailpipe, heater not working, temperature gauge fluctuation

The 928's V8 produces significant heat and relies on a complex cooling system. The water pump, driven by the timing belt, is a common failure point. The radiator's plastic end tanks crack with age. The thermostat can fail open or closed. Coolant hoses, now 25-45 years old, become brittle and collapse internally. The heater core, buried in the dashboard, can leak or clog. Head gasket failure, while less common, can cause coolant consumption and overheating.

Common Fixes

  • Replace water pump with every timing belt service
  • Replace thermostat with OEM temperature rating
  • Replace radiator if plastic tanks are cracking or leaking
  • Replace all coolant hoses (including heater hoses)
  • Flush cooling system and use proper G05 or Porsche-spec coolant
  • Check and clean electric cooling fan operation
  • Pressure test system to find hidden leaks

Sources: Pelican Parts, Rennlist

Vacuum System Deterioration

Symptoms: Climate control not working, doors won't lock, cruise control failure, rough idle, brake booster issues

The 928 uses vacuum to power many systems including HVAC controls, door locks (on early cars), cruise control, and brake booster. Over 25-45 years, the vacuum hoses become brittle and crack, causing leaks. The vacuum reservoir and check valves can fail. Vacuum leaks cause a cascade of seemingly unrelated failures - non-functional climate control, door locks that don't work, and rough running from unmetered air entering the engine. Tracking down vacuum leaks is a common diagnostic challenge on 928s.

Common Fixes

  • Replace all vacuum hoses throughout the car
  • Check and replace vacuum reservoir if cracked
  • Replace vacuum check valves
  • Test vacuum actuators in doors and HVAC system
  • Replace brake booster check valve if brakes feel hard
  • Use proper vacuum line material (silicone hose for engine, rubber for interior)
  • Create vacuum diagram documentation for future reference

Sources: 928 Specialists, Rennlist

Weissach Axle Suspension Wear (S4/GTS)

Symptoms: Rear-end wander, unpredictable handling at limit, clunking from rear, uneven rear tire wear

Later 928 models (S4, GT, GTS) use the Weissach rear axle, a sophisticated multi-link suspension that provides passive rear-wheel steering for improved handling. The system uses multiple bushings and control arms that wear over time. Worn Weissach axle components cause unpredictable handling characteristics that undermine the car's exceptional dynamics. The system's complexity makes diagnosis and repair more involved than a conventional rear suspension.

Common Fixes

  • Inspect all Weissach axle bushings for wear
  • Replace control arm bushings (polyurethane or OEM rubber available)
  • Replace toe links and trailing arm bushings
  • Check wheel bearings for play
  • Rebuild or replace control arms if ball joints are worn
  • Perform full rear alignment after suspension work
  • Consider specialist shops familiar with Weissach axle geometry

Sources: 928 International, Rennlist

Pop-Up Headlight Failure

Symptoms: Headlights stuck up or down, slow headlight operation, one headlight won't open, motor noise but no movement

The 928's distinctive pop-up headlights are operated by electric motors driving cables and linkages. The motors can fail, the cables can break or stretch, and the plastic linkage components can crack. The headlight pods themselves can stick due to dried lubricant or damaged pivot points. When the headlights fail, they typically fail in the down position, leaving you without headlights - a safety concern.

Common Fixes

  • Lubricate headlight pivot points with white lithium grease
  • Replace headlight cables if stretched or frayed
  • Replace headlight motor if failed (test with direct 12V first)
  • Replace plastic linkage components if cracked
  • Adjust limit switches for proper open/close positions
  • Check relay and switch operation
  • Keep spare motors - used units often available from parted cars

Sources: Rennlist, Pelican Parts

Instrument Cluster and Electronics

Symptoms: Dead or flickering gauges, inaccurate readings, warning lights stuck on, odometer failure, dim display

The 928's instrument cluster is a complex electronic unit with multiple displays and warning systems. The circuits board develops solder cracks from thermal cycling. Capacitors fail with age. The LCD displays (on later models) can fade or fail. The odometer uses the same plastic gear issue as the 944. The 928 also has early electronic systems for climate control and trip computer that develop problems with age. Finding a fully functional 928 cluster is increasingly difficult.

Common Fixes

  • Re-solder cracked connections on cluster circuit board
  • Replace failed capacitors (common cause of gauge issues)
  • Replace LCD displays with new or NOS units
  • Replace odometer gears (brass upgrade kits available)
  • Have cluster professionally rebuilt by 928 specialist
  • Check all ground connections between cluster and chassis
  • Replace bulbs with LED for improved longevity (where applicable)

Sources: 928 Specialists, Rennlist

Air Conditioning System Failures

Symptoms: No cold air, weak airflow, hot on one side and cold on the other, clicking from dashboard, refrigerant leaks

The 928's automatic climate control system was advanced for its time but is now 25-45 years old. The compressor, condenser, and evaporator all wear out. The early R12 systems have been converted to R134a, often improperly. The servo motors that control temperature blend doors fail, causing temperature issues. The heater core can leak. The system uses vacuum to control some functions, adding to potential failure points. Many 928s have non-functional or poorly functioning A/C.

Common Fixes

  • Properly convert R12 systems to R134a (requires correct oil and seals)
  • Replace compressor if failed or worn
  • Replace condenser and receiver/drier if system was open
  • Repair or replace evaporator if leaking
  • Replace blend door servo motors (accessible with dash removal)
  • Check vacuum supply to HVAC system
  • Clean and flush system before recharging

Sources: Rennlist, 928 International
